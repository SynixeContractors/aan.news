Pictured: A Te Manava Roa Leader

Te Manava Roa: We Are Ready

By Paiea Tama

It's a regular Friday night, I'm working next to a cup of coffee and a half-eaten sandwich. My phone rings, unknown number. I pick up, and it's a member of Te Manava Roa, he wants to take me to a meeting with one of the leaders of the group.

Three hours later, I'm in the back of a van, I'm blindfolded. We pull into a long barn, and I'm taken out of the van. The blindfold is removed, and I'm standing in front of a man in a yellow shirt. He introduces himself as the leader of Te Manava Roa.

We speak for only a few minutes, but his message is clear. Te Manava Roa is angry, and they're ready.

He briefly tells me about the group's history, formed in 2029, after the island became fully independent from France. The group was formed to restore the island's culture and traditions, after nearly 120 years of French rule.

That may sound different than how they group has previously been portrayed, as a group of radicals, a criminal organization with their hands in smuggling all kinds of illicit goods. But the leader tells me that's not the whole story. "We are not criminals," he says. "We are not monsters. We are the true people of this island, and we are ready to fight for our land. Te Manava Roa has people in every village, every town, in the cities, in the government, in the Gendarmerie. We are everywhere."

I asked about the last few days, the reports of gunfire, the house of a now missing Gendarmerie officer being burned down, a mine owner on the run, a dead farm owner and an island in chaos. "Mr. Fairchild is here to eat up our land, he's swooping in with violent thugs, and we will not stand for it," he says. "We did not want any violence, but we will watch our homeland burn before we let it be taken from us again." The leader seems to be referencing the social media posts that went viral, showing security contractors, armed, near an indigenous village, with Mr. Fairchild nowhere in sight.

Just as quickly as the meeting started, it was over. I was blindfold and back home. Te Manava Road is ready, and they wanted the people to know it.

I contacted the office of the Gendarmerie Commander for comment. "We are aware of rising instances of gun violence on our islands, and we are working to bring the perpetrators to justice." a spokesperson said, "We urge all citizens to remain calm, report suspicious activity, and to not take the law into their own hands. We have multiple open investigations, and we are working with elected government to ensure peaceful and just resolutions to these issues."
